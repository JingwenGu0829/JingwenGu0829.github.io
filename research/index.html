Ladder-BAI: Beating Preference Saturation with Warm-Started Best-Arm Identification

Undergraduate Math-Computer Science Honors Thesis

In preference-based reinforcement learning, the Bradley–Terry model maps reward differences through a logistic link, causing the Fisher information of each comparison to decay as e-|Δ| when the learner is compared against a fixed, weak reference. This preference saturation effect can make sample complexity scale exponentially in the reward range Rmax. We study this problem in the K-armed dueling bandit setting and propose Ladder-BAI, an algorithm that periodically promotes the baseline arm to the current empirical best, keeping all comparisons in the informative regime of the sigmoid. Our main result shows that Ladder-BAI identifies an ε-optimal arm with probability 1-δ using Õ(KRmax + K/ε²) preference queries—linear in Rmax, an exponential improvement over fixed-baseline methods and a polynomial improvement over the O(Rmax5/2) bound known for general function approximation. The proof relies on a reward-ladder argument: each epoch yields a constant reward gain via Hoeffding concentration and logistic link inversion, so O(Rmax) epochs suffice to reach the local regime where standard best-arm identification applies. Experiments on synthetic Bradley–Terry bandits confirm the predicted linear scaling in Rmax and K.
